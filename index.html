§ 1

Why Two People Get Different Results

⏱ 17 min

The gap between a useless AI response and a brilliant one almost never comes down to which model you use. It comes down to how you ask. That skill has a name: prompt engineering.

Prompt Engineering

Crafting your input to an AI model to maximize the quality, relevance, and usefulness of the output. Not tricking the AI — communicating clearly with it.

💬
Analogy · The Vague Bug Report

"The model isn't working" vs "validation accuracy dropped from 85% to 61% right after last week's retrain — can you check if the new training data is leaking future info into the features?" Both ask for help. One gets an answer in ten minutes. The other wastes a day just decoding what you meant.

Quiz 1 · Why did Prompt B produce dramatically better output than Prompt A, even though both used the same model and same source notes?

APrompt B used a more powerful AI model behind the scenes
BPrompt B was longer, and longer prompts always produce better results
CPrompt B provided clear structure — role, context, format, and constraints — that guided the model's output

Why C? Length alone doesn't help — a 500-word vague prompt still returns garbage. What matters is structure and specificity. Prompt B told the model WHO it was, WHAT it was working with, WHAT format to use, and WHAT rules to follow. Same AI, dramatically different outcome.
§ 2

The RCTFC Framework

⏱ 20 min

What made Prompt B better? A five-part structure that works for every prompt you'll ever write: Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints.

🍽️
Analogy · Ordering at a Restaurant

"Give me food" vs "the grilled paneer tikka (Task), medium spice (Constraint), rice not naan (Format), I'm allergic to nuts (Context), and I trust your chef's seasoning (Role)." Same kitchen, wildly different meal. AI responds to specificity the same way.

📋
Second Analogy · Writing a Job Posting

A good JD names the role ("Senior Backend Engineer"), gives context (team, product stage), states the task (responsibilities), fixes a format (resume + portfolio link), and sets constraints (experience range, salary band). A vague JD gets you fifty mismatched resumes. A precise one gets you three great ones. Same five ingredients, same effect on an AI prompt.

RCTFC Framework

A five-part structure for effective prompts: Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints. Together they give the AI everything it needs to produce exactly what you want.

Danger case — contradictory constraints: Aisha writes Task: "include the full raw transcript of every speaker" and Constraints: "under 500 words." The model can't do both — it either blows past the word limit or silently drops the transcript, and you won't know which without checking. RCTFC only helps when its five elements agree with each other.
Beginner note: You don't need all five elements every time. For "What does overfitting mean?", just the Task is enough. For anything needing more than a one-line answer, RCTFC is your go-to.
Pro note: The five elements don't have to appear in R-C-T-F-C order. Some of the best prompts open with the Task and close with Constraints. What matters is that all five show up somewhere — not the sequence.
Advanced note: In production, Role + Context + Constraints are often encoded as a persistent system prompt that stays constant across interactions, while Task + Format change per request.
In the industry: OpenAI's own developer docs teach prompt structure around this same idea — role, context, and constraints up front. RCTFC just adds an explicit Role slot because it matters even more once a prompt goes into a real product.
§ 3

Few-Shot Prompting + Output Formatting

⏱ 30 min

RCTFC gives structure. But when output must match a very specific format — your team's template — instructions alone can't capture the nuance. Instead of describing the format, you SHOW it. Both few-shot and format control are about the same thing: controlling the shape of the output.

👥
Analogy · Onboarding a New Teammate

Telling a new hire "write it up clearly" is zero-shot — they guess. Showing them three of your team's best experiment write-ups and saying "document it like this" is few-shot. Same person, dramatically better first draft.

🎨
Second Analogy · Style Transfer

Words often fail where examples succeed. You can't fully describe "paint like Van Gogh" in a sentence — but hand an artist three Van Gogh paintings and say "more like this," and they get it instantly. Few-shot prompting is the same move: stop describing the style, show it.

Zero-Shot

Instructions without any examples. You describe what you want; the model picks a format on its own.

Few-Shot

2–3 examples of the exact output you want, then the task. The examples teach the model your specific style.

Danger case — inconsistent examples: Aisha grabs 3 old action-item messages fast — but one uses "Owner:", another uses "Assigned to:", and the third skips priority entirely. Few-shot with inconsistent examples doesn't average them into a clean format — it confuses the model into blending fields unpredictably. Bad examples are worse than no examples.

Advanced note: More examples isn't always better. Past ~5 examples, the model can start over-fitting to quirks of your specific examples rather than the general pattern. 2-3 well-chosen examples is the sweet spot for most tasks.
In the industry: Zomato's support bot reportedly uses few-shot prompting with 3-5 previous ticket resolutions as examples — that's how a generic model ends up sounding like it always has.

Quiz 2 · Your team has a specific Model Card format with sections like "Intended Use," "Training Data," "Performance," and "Limitations" in a very particular style. You want the AI to produce one in exactly this format. Which approach is most effective?

AZero-shot: describe the format in words and trust the model to figure it out

Why B? When output must match a specific template, showing beats telling. You don't hand a new teammate a 20-page style guide — you show them three great model cards and say "do it like this."

Few-shot solved the template problem for one audience. But Aisha's notes need to reach four different audiences — a Slack update, a sprint tracker, a JSON payload for another tool, and a formal model card. Same information, four shapes. That's output formatting.

Output Formatting

Explicitly defining the structure, shape, and style of the AI's response. Like writing SELECT customer_id, churn_score FROM customers instead of "give me customer data" — you get exactly what you specified.

🔌
Second Analogy · The API Contract

The AI is your junior dev. You wouldn't say "give me some data" and hope — you'd say "return JSON with these 5 fields, these types, this key order." Output formatting is that same rigor, applied to a chat model instead of a function signature.

Danger case — over-specified format: Aisha defines a JSON schema with 20 required fields for a 3-sentence status update. The source material only supports 6 of them. Rather than leaving fields blank, the model invents plausible-sounding values to fill the schema. Format control only helps when the shape you ask for matches the substance you actually have.
Model Card

A short, standardized document describing an ML model — what it's for, what data it trained on, how well it performs, and its limitations. So anyone can understand what a model does and doesn't do before relying on it.

Pro note: Production teams often use JSON schema validators to check the AI's output actually matches the schema they asked for — and auto-retry if it doesn't. That's part of how tools like Notion AI avoid ever returning a broken format to the user.
In the industry: Every AI feature you use day to day — Notion AI, Grammarly, Linear's AI triage — controls output shape exactly this way. There's no other way to get a chat model to reliably return structured data.
§ 4

Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

⏱ 13 min

Priya's follow-up isn't a formatting ask — it's a reasoning ask. Some tasks need the AI to actually think through a problem, not just organize text that's already there.

🦆
Analogy · Rubber Duck Debugging

When you're stuck, explaining the problem step by step to a rubber duck often reveals the answer. Articulating your reasoning catches errors you'd miss by jumping to a conclusion. LLMs work the same way — tell the model to think step by step and the reasoning itself improves.

➗
Second Analogy · Long Division on Paper

You can't do 847 × 293 reliably in your head — but write out the intermediate steps and the answer gets much more reliable. The LLM is no different: forcing it to externalize intermediate steps measurably improves the odds the final answer is right.

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

Explicitly asking the AI to reason through a problem step by step before its final answer. The model "shows its work" — and that process produces better results.

Danger case — CoT on a task that doesn't need it: Ask "think step by step" before "translate 'churn' to Hindi" and you get a paragraph of unnecessary deliberation before a one-word answer — slower, and sometimes the reasoning talks the model into second-guessing a perfectly correct instinct. Reserve CoT for tasks that actually require multiple reasoning steps.

Plausible but shallow. Three items, no ranking, no reasoning for why any one is "most urgent." Misses cascade chains and the rollback plan nobody discussed.

🧠 Chain-of-Thought Answer
  • Step 1 — Map: Feature list (Apr 20), Prototype (Apr 25), Holdout (TBD), Dashboards (Apr 22), Rollback (undiscussed)
  • Step 2 — Dependency chains: Feature list → Training → Prototype → Holdout → Go-live
  • Step 3 — Flag ownerless items: Monitoring comparison doc has no owner and no clear deadline
  • Step 4 — Trace cascades: Feature list slippage cascades through 4 downstream stages; monitoring gap blocks dashboards → alerting → go-live readiness
  • Step 5 — What's missing: Rollback plan was never discussed at all
  • Final answer: The feature list is the single highest-blast-radius risk — a slip on Apr 20 delays everything downstream of it. Second: the ownerless monitoring doc, since nobody is accountable for unblocking it.

The steps are visible in the output — CoT didn't just relabel the same list, it produced a ranked, reasoned answer no direct prompt gave us.

1

Map every task and its deadline onto a timeline

2

Identify which tasks depend on other tasks completing first — draw the dependency chains

3

Flag any task with no owner, no deadline, or an unresolved decision blocking it

4

Trace which delays cascade and affect the most downstream work

5

Consider what was NOT discussed but should have been

Rule of thumb: If a good answer requires more than one mental step, add chain-of-thought. If you'd whiteboard it before answering, tell the AI to whiteboard it too.
Beginner note: The simplest way to trigger CoT is the literal phrase "think step by step" before your question. Try it once on any multi-step question — you'll see the difference immediately.
In the industry: Anthropic's Claude was one of the first models to bake this in natively — they call it "extended thinking." The technique you just used by hand is the same one, just automated.

§ 5

Refining a Prompt in Five Rounds

⏱ 17 min

Almost nobody writes a great prompt on the first try. Prompt refinement IS debugging: write, read the output, identify the failure, fix one thing, run again.

🐞
Analogy · Debugging, Not Dictation

Nobody ships their first draft of a model. Don't ship your first draft of a prompt. Aisha needs to email Priya a summary of her action items, flag concerns, and propose a plan. Watch it evolve.

📷
Second Analogy · A Photographer's First Shot

No photographer ships the first frame. They adjust framing, then lighting, then focus — same subject, dramatically different result by the fifth shot. A prompt improves the same way: one deliberate adjustment per round, not a random reshoot each time.

In the industry: GitHub's Copilot team has said publicly that their best internal prompts are on version 40-plus. Prompts are code — version them, iterate on them, don't expect version 1 to ship.

Quiz 4 · Aisha gets output that is factually correct and well-structured, but the tone is too formal for an internal Slack message — it reads like a legal document. What is the most effective next step?

ARewrite the entire prompt from scratch with a different approach
BAdd a constraint specifying the desired tone (e.g., "casual, peer-to-peer, like a Slack message")
CSwitch to a different AI model that naturally writes more casually
DManually edit the output to fix the tone
Why B? The output was close — correct facts, good structure — so the fix is surgical, not a rewrite. Diagnose what's wrong (tone too formal), fix that one thing (add a tone constraint), run again. Exactly like debugging a model: you don't retrain from scratch because one hyperparameter is off.

§ 7

Wrap-Up — The Prompt Engineer's Checklist

⏱ 5 min

Five takeaways to reference every time you write a prompt.

1 · RCTFC anatomy

Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints. Same principle as a well-written project brief: specificity in, quality out.

2 · Zero-shot vs few-shot

When output must match a template, show 2-3 examples. The examples ARE the instruction.

3 · Chain-of-thought

For analysis or multi-step reasoning, tell the AI to think step by step. Rubber duck debugging applied to AI.

4 · Output formatting

Define output like a prediction schema: exact fields, headers, keys. If you can sketch it in 10 seconds, the sketch belongs in the prompt.

5 · Iterative refinement

Prompting is debugging, not dictation. Write, read, diagnose, fix one thing, repeat. Nobody ships their first version of a model. Don't ship your first draft of a prompt.
